Looper

Record mic input, loop it back against the running progression. Opt-in, off by default, and only available when cycle === 'none' — a fixed-length loop would clash against a key that shifts every lap. Today each section can hold one loop; Phase 3 (see Multi-loop data model) lifts that to several layered loops per section.

Background

Lets a player record themselves (or an instrument) over the progression and hear it loop back on top of the chords/bass/drums — practicing over your own take, not just the backing track.

Engine lives in src/progression-audio.ts, exposed through the AudioEngine interface in src/progression-core.ts, with UI/state glue in src/app.ts — same three-file split as the rest of the app (see ARCHITECTURE.md).

What's built

Record, play, delete

One loop per section, no overdub — delete-and-rerecord is the only edit. State machine idle → arming → recording; "does section X have a loop" is a derived read (sections[X].loops.length > 0), not a state value.

The sync boundary is section entry, not the whole-song lap: ev.chipIndex === 0 inside _buildPart's Tone.Part callback fires every time a section starts, including a manual-hold repeat of the same section. Arming confines playback to the active section immediately (see below), so the count-in always lands on that section's own bar 1; recording finalizes on that section's next entry, so the loop's bar 1 lines up with its owning section's bar 1. ChordTickEvent.sectionChanged was considered and rejected as the signal — it's Draw-scheduled/UI-facing and deliberately doesn't re-fire on a manual-hold repeat, which is exactly the case a loop restart needs to fire on.

Playback force-restarts on every section entry — player.stop(time); player.start(time), same pattern as drum sample triggering — no Player.loop/.sync(), so it can never drift relative to the Transport. A tempo change after capture degrades gracefully (truncates early or leaves a trailing gap) rather than drifting. One shared Tone.Player serves whichever section is currently active, swapping buffers by loop id as sections change.

Capture: Tone.UserMedia → Tone.Recorder, scheduled via Tone.Draw.schedule. Post-record: decode, then trim/zero-pad to exactly the recording section's own bar length.

Confined playback while recording

Arming forces the transport into a hold on the target section — its own chords/bass/drums, repeating — instead of continuing through the arrangement; the hold rides through arm → record → looping and only releases on cancel or stop. Built on the manual-advance "hold on this section" primitive already used by _advance === "manual"; arming stashes the real setting in _preHoldAdvance and forces manual, restoring it on cancel or stop. Both scrubbers and the Advance pill lock (pill reads "🔒 Held") for the duration — a scrubber tap during a hold would otherwise queue a jump that breaks confinement the moment the hold's own boundary-check runs.

Mute during recording

Optional toggle for players without headphones: silences chord/bass/drum audio (not the loop channel — no loop has siblings yet, that's a Phase 3 concern) for the recording window only. A transient setMute override per channel, restored to whatever state.mix already had afterward — never touches AppState.

Manual sync offset

Mic/input-chain latency varies per device and isn't something the app can measure — a "Loop sync offset (ms)" field in Setup lets the player dial it in by ear once (measured range so far: ~50ms on a laptop, ~350ms on a phone). A single global/per-device value, not per-loop — it corrects mic/input-chain latency, a device property, not a content property.

Each loop's decoded recording is cached by id, so nudging the offset re-derives the trimmed buffer without re-recording, via a pure function: 

alignAndTrimSamples(data: Float32Array, targetLength: number, offsetSamples = 0): Float32Array

AudioEngine.setLoopOffsetMs(ms) immediately re-trims whichever loop is currently loaded into the shared player; other sections' loops pick up the new offset lazily, next time they're swapped in. Persisted in localStorage like the other looper settings.

Persistence

Metadata (LoopRef, keyed by the loop's id) lives on Section.loops and rides through the normal AppState/preset boot path. The audio blob stays in IndexedDB, one row per loop id (keyPath: "id", not localStorage — blobs are binary, a few hundred KB each). Whichever section is entered during playback fetches its own blob by id (cached in memory after first fetch). Deleting a section's loop (deleteLoop(sectionIndex)) removes its IndexedDB row; other sections are untouched.

Gotcha: a plain browser refresh does not replay preset-loading — it rebuilds sections from URL params, which never carry loops. mergeSectionLoops(sections) backfills .loops onto the URL-derived sections by index right after preset-context is set in app.ts's boot sequence. Any new boot path needs to call this too, or loops silently fail to restore.

Mixer & cleanup effects

A 4th Tone.Channel (loop) gives the loop independent volume/mute in the Mix sheet, gated by the feature flag rather than cycle === 'none'. The Looper group in the Mix sheet reflects whichever section is active/selected in the scrubber — reading from and writing to that section's LoopRef (setLoopVolume/setLoopMuted/etc. all take a sectionIndex, routed through _updateSectionLoop); every control disables/dims when the selected section has no loop. Three optional cleanup effects sit between the player and that channel:

  • Tone.Filter (fixed 90Hz highpass) — cuts mic rumble/handling noise
  • Tone.Compressor — single 0-100 "amount" slider mapping threshold+ratio together
  • Tone.Limiter (fixed -3dB) — catches occasional clipping

Highpass/limiter are true bypasses — physically removed from the graph when off — so toggling one is a clean A/B. _rewireLoopChain() rebuilds the chain on every toggle. The engine still has only one shared player/effects chain (Phase 3's job to change), so a section's settings are applied (_applyLoopSettingsToChain) the moment its buffer swaps in.

Feature flag & UI

looperEnabled, a localStorage app-level setting, in its own "Looper (Experimental)" Setup section — defaults off, and the loop UI doesn't render at all when off. One button (#loop-btn) in the action bar, label combining LooperState with whether the active section already has a loop: idle+no-loop "⏺ Record Loop", arming "⏳ Get Ready…" (tap = cancel), recording "● Recording…" (tap = cancel), idle+has-loop "🗑 Delete Loop" (behind a confirm() dialog). Hidden when the flag is off or cycle !== 'none'.

Multi-loop data model

Unreleased — backward compatibility is not a goal, for any phase of this build. Only the developer is dogfooding this; nobody else has recorded loops that need to keep working. If a cleaner shape requires breaking the stored data format, an existing setter signature, or in-progress recordings, just break it — bump the IndexedDB version and wipe the store, change a method signature, whatever's cleanest. Don't spend effort on migration code, optional/fallback fields, or preserving old behavior "just in case." 
interface LoopRef {
  id: string;            // uuid — IndexedDB key for the raw recorded blob
  label?: string;        // optional; UI falls back to "Loop N" by array position
  capturedBars: number;  // this section's bar length at capture time
  volume: number;        // 0-100, per-loop
  muted: boolean;
  compression: number;   // 0-100, per-loop
  highpass: boolean;
  limiter: boolean;
}

interface Section {
  progression: string;
  loops: LoopRef[];      // Phases 1-2: capped at 1. Phase 3 lifts the cap —
                         // ALL non-muted loops play together.
}

Decisions that hold across all phases and constrain how Phase 3 gets built:

Layered, not single-active-loop. Every non-muted loop on a section plays at once — a loop-pedal feel, not a track picker. (Today's cap of 1 makes this moot in practice; Phase 3 is where it matters.)
Sync boundary is section entry (chipIndex === 0), not the whole-song lap or ChordTickEvent.sectionChanged. See Record, play, delete for why the latter was rejected. Side effect worth remembering: every occurrence of a repeated section replays the same loop(s) by default — there's no per-instance override, and none is planned unless requested.
Storage: metadata on Section, blobs in IndexedDB keyed by a flat loop id (not partitioned by song/preset). copyLoop(id) deep-copies a blob under a fresh id — used by Save-As so no two presets ever share an id, which is what keeps deleteLoop()'s unconditional delete safe with no reference counting. GC is a periodic sweep (live state ∪ every saved preset → delete anything in IndexedDB not referenced), not cascade-on-delete — not yet implemented, orphan rows just accumulate today. Sync offset stays one global/per-device setting, not per-loop.
Local-only, deliberately. Loops never travel through the URL (binary can't fit); a preset's loop ids are meaningless without matching IndexedDB rows on that browser, so presets carrying loops are effectively device-local for audio content.
Deferred: section duplication. No duplicateSection() exists today, so what happens to a duplicated section's loops (empty / deep-copy / alias the id) is unanswered. Doesn't change the structures above either way — revisit only if that feature gets proposed.

Phase 3 — lift the one-loop-per-section cap

The actual multi-loop feature, split into stepping stones — sequenced so 3a-3c ship with no visible UX change, verified before the cap actually lifts in 3d.

3a — implement the GC sweep for real. Implemented, pending manual verification. Independent of the N-loop work below — pure IndexedDB hygiene: collect every loop id referenced by live state ∪ every saved preset, delete anything else, run at existing checkpoints (deleteLoop, removeSection, deleteUserPreset, overwriteUserPreset). AudioEngine.sweepOrphanedLoops(keepIds) does the actual IndexedDB diff/delete and evicts swept ids from the in-memory buffer cache; progression-core.ts's _referencedLoopIds() computes the keep-set since it's the only side that can see presets. Landing this first means the N-loop work that follows doesn't compound an already-known gap. Not wired to run on app boot — only the checkpoints above — so any orphans already sitting in IndexedDB from before this landed won't clear until one of them runs the sweep. overwriteUserPreset ("Save," not "Save As") is the trickiest checkpoint: a loop deleted or a section removed doesn't actually become unreachable until the preset holding the old reference is re-saved with the new, smaller state — the sweep at removeSection/deleteLoop time correctly leaves it alone if the loaded preset on disk still references it, so overwriteUserPreset has to be its own checkpoint rather than relying on the other three to eventually catch it.
3b — engine capacity: N players/effects chains per section. Widen the engine from one shared player to a true array per section; likely where the looper gets its own module (progression-looper.ts, out of progression-audio.ts). Also where buffer priming lands — the inherited async-swap gap (see Record, play, delete) gets worse with N buffers instead of 1, so solve it here rather than bolt it on later. State-machine cap stays at 1 — invisible, verified against today's one-loop-per-section behavior. Prioritized to land early, since it's the biggest re-architecture and most likely to surface issues worth knowing about before the rest is built on top of it.
3c — mixer becomes a list of rows. UI renders N rows (today always 1, so visually identical) — gets per-loop volume/mute/compression/highpass/limiter controls in place before recording a second loop is even possible.
3d — lift the record cap; mute-during-recording extends to siblings; delete gets a flyout. The real visible feature: arming when a loop already exists on the section adds a new layer instead of blocking. "Silence everything" (see Mute during recording) now also silences existing loops during a new capture. Delete UI: with exactly one loop, the button behaves exactly as it does today (immediate confirm()); with more than one, tapping it opens a flyout listing each loop (falling back to "Loop N" by array position, per the existing label rule) so the player can pick which one to remove — each row still behind its own confirm.

What this enables today

  • Different sections in the same song can each carry their own loop — no longer one loop for the whole song
  • Per-loop volume/mute/compression/highpass/limiter, tuned by selecting a section in the scrubber and opening the Mix sheet
  • Loops saved and reloaded as part of a preset, alongside the progression they belong to
  • Every occurrence of a repeated section plays the same loop automatically
  • Recording plays back just the section being worked on, not the whole song, until you stop
  • A one-click "silence everything" option for recording without headphones

Limitations / tradeoffs

  • Still one loop per section — Phase 3 lifts this; no layering yet
  • No per-instance override — all occurrences of a repeated section share the same loop
  • While recording, you only hear the section being worked on — no live preview against the rest of the song until you stop and press play again
  • Loops don't travel through shared URLs — a shared link carries chords/arrangement only
  • Presets carrying loop metadata are device-local — the audio doesn't move with the preset across browsers/machines
  • Deleted loops' IndexedDB rows aren't reclaimed immediately — no GC sweep yet

Other ideas under consideration

Smaller, independent of the data model above — each is a toggle-and-judge experiment, not a commitment.

  • Loop-seam declick — not prioritized, no reported clicking yet; if revisited, snap trim points to the nearest zero-crossing rather than fading amplitude (a fade risks touching beat 1 or sounding like an audible cut on beat 4).
  • Raw mic constraints vs. our cleanup effects — A/B getUserMedia's built-in echoCancellation/noiseSuppression/autoGainControl against our own hi-pass/compressor before trusting either verdict.
  • Recording codec/bitrate — Tone.Recorder's mimeType option is unused (browser default); worth checking if it's limiting quality.
  • Auto level-match — normalize recorded peak/RMS automatically so the volume slider doesn't need manual re-riding per take.
  • Tempo-following playback (time-stretch) — swap Tone.Player for Tone.GrainPlayer, track tempo-at-capture, and recompute playbackRate on tempo change instead of truncating/gapping. Real risk: granular stretching can sound warbly on transient-heavy material or large tempo swings.
